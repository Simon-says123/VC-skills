# Startup Evaluation Framework

**Purpose:** Guides the deep research phase of the evaluating-startups skill. Each dimension below defines what to look for, what good looks like, and what red flags to surface. This framework is applied in Step 2 (Deep Research) and informs both the Evaluation Report and the Opportunity Brief.

**Scope:** More rigorous than an initial screen. Every dimension should be researched with specific evidence — named customers, cited metrics, verified funding data, linked sources. Label all inferences and unverified claims.

---

## Dimension 1 — Team

**What to look for:**
- Founder-market fit: does the founding team have the domain expertise, network, or lived experience that gives them an unfair advantage in this market?
- Prior exits or venture-backed experience: have they built and scaled a company before?
- Technical vs. commercial balance: is there strong product/engineering leadership alongside go-to-market capability?
- Hiring velocity and quality: are they attracting strong senior talent? VP-level hires in the last 6–12 months are a proxy for trajectory.
- Advisory network: who is advising them and what does it signal about their access to capital, customers, or expertise?

**What good looks like:**
- Founders with deep domain expertise, strong operator track records, or prior exits in the category
- Recent VP/C-suite hires from credible companies
- Advisors who are active (board seats, known involvement) not decorative

**Red flags:**
- Thin or misaligned founding team (e.g., all technical, no commercial; all commercial, no product)
- High early leadership churn
- Founders with no prior experience in the sector and no clear compensating credential

---

## Dimension 2 — Product and Technology

**What to look for:**
- Core product: what does it actually do, and how does it work at a functional level?
- Differentiation: what makes this product meaningfully different from alternatives — and is that difference durable?
- Technical moat: is the advantage rooted in data, proprietary algorithms, network effects, switching costs, or IP?
- Build status: is the product live, in pilot, or still in development? What is the current depth of functionality?
- Roadmap signals: what is the product trajectory and does it suggest a defensible category leader or a point solution?

**What good looks like:**
- A clear, demonstrable technical advantage that competitors cannot easily replicate
- Evidence of product-led growth or strong stickiness signals (low churn, high engagement, long contracts)
- IP, proprietary data, or network effects that compound over time

**Red flags:**
- Product that could be replicated by a well-resourced competitor in 12–18 months
- Heavy reliance on third-party data or APIs without a clear path to proprietary advantage
- Feature product masquerading as a platform

---

## Dimension 3 — Traction

**What to look for:**
- Revenue: ARR, MRR, growth rate, and trajectory. Is revenue recurring?
- Customer base: who are the named customers, at what scale, and at what ACV?
- Pipeline quality: LOIs, pilots, or signed contracts in progress
- Retention signals: NRR, churn rates, expansion revenue
- Usage and engagement: for product-led businesses, DAU/MAU, feature adoption, activation rates

**What good looks like:**
- Clear revenue growth with consistent MoM or QoQ trajectory
- Named enterprise customers or credible logos, especially any recognisable insurers or financial institutions
- NRR above 110% indicating expansion within existing accounts
- Conversion from pilot to paid at a healthy rate

**Red flags:**
- Revenue plateau or growth deceleration without a clear explanation
- Unnamed or anonymised customer base with no verifiable proof points
- Heavy concentration in one or two customers
- Long sales cycles without evidence of acceleration

---

## Dimension 4 — Market

**What to look for:**
- TAM: is the total addressable market large enough to support a venture-scale outcome ($1B+ revenue potential)?
- SAM and SOM: is the serviceable market realistic and is the initial beachhead well-defined?
- Growth dynamics: what is driving market growth — regulatory change, technology adoption, demographic shift, structural risk change?
- Timing: why now? What has changed in the last 2–3 years that makes this the right moment?
- Geographic scope: is the model replicable across markets or locked to one jurisdiction?

**What good looks like:**
- A large, growing market with identifiable tailwinds that are structural rather than cyclical
- A clear, narrow initial beachhead that expands logically into the broader TAM
- Evidence that the timing is right: recent regulatory changes, technology enablers, or customer behaviour shifts

**Red flags:**
- Market size inflated by including segments the company cannot realistically address
- No clear thesis on why now — market has existed for years without adoption
- Regulatory risk that could fundamentally constrain growth

---

## Dimension 5 — Business Model

**What to look for:**
- Pricing model: SaaS, usage-based, transactional, commission? Is it recurring or lumpy?
- ACV and deal size: what is the average contract value and does it support the growth model?
- GTM motion: product-led, sales-led, or partner-led? Is it working?
- Sales cycle length: how long from first contact to closed contract?
- Unit economics: what do gross margins look like? What is the estimated LTV/CAC ratio?
- Burn and runway: how much are they spending and how long until they need to raise again?

**What good looks like:**
- High gross margins (70%+ for SaaS) with a credible path to operating leverage
- A GTM motion that is working and scalable — not just founder-led sales
- ACV that supports the sales model (low ACV + high-touch sales = structural problem)
- 18+ months of runway post-current round

**Red flags:**
- Unclear or inconsistent pricing model
- Negative gross margins with no credible path to improvement
- Over-reliance on founder-led sales without a repeatable GTM playbook
- Burn rate that implies a very short runway post-raise

---

## Dimension 6 — Competitive Position

**What to look for:**
- Who are the top 3–5 direct competitors and what is the basis of competition?
- Where does this company sit on the competitive map — category leader, fast follower, or niche player?
- What is the moat relative to each competitor (price, product, data, distribution, brand)?
- Are incumbents (large tech, insurers, banks) a threat? Could they replicate or acquire their way into this space?
- What is the switching cost for a customer moving from this product to a competitor?

**What good looks like:**
- A demonstrable advantage over the top 2–3 named competitors on at least one dimension that matters to buyers
- Switching costs that lock in customers once they onboard (data integration, workflow dependency, network effects)
- A credible answer to the "why not just build this internally?" question

**Red flags:**
- Commodity product with no durable differentiation
- Well-funded, fast-moving competitors gaining ground
- Category that large incumbents (Salesforce, AWS, major insurers) are moving into with native capability

---

## Dimension 7 — Investor Quality and Round Dynamics

**What to look for:**
- Who has led prior rounds? What does their conviction signal about the company's trajectory?
- Who else is in the cap table — strategic investors, CVCs, tier-1 VCs?
- Is the current round properly structured — realistic valuation, sensible use of proceeds?
- Is there a credible lead for the current round, or is it being assembled ad hoc?
- What is the dilution profile for existing investors?

**What good looks like:**
- Prior rounds led by tier-1 VCs or credible domain-specific investors
- Clean cap table without excessive dilution or complex instruments
- A lead investor committed or in advanced diligence for the current round

**Red flags:**
- Prior rounds full of angels and family offices with no institutional lead
- Messy cap table — too many small investors, complicated instruments, or high existing dilution
- Round being shopped widely with no credible lead emerging

---

## Dimension 8 — Insurance and Strategic Relevance

**What to look for:**
- Where in the insurance value chain does this company sit — underwriting, pricing, distribution, claims, customer experience, risk prevention?
- Which specific IAG business units (RIA, IIA, NZ, Group) could benefit and how?
- What is the mechanism for value creation — cost reduction, revenue growth, risk improvement, capability building?
- Is there a credible pilot pathway within 6–12 months, or is this longer-horizon strategic optionality?
- Are there other insurers already using or piloting this technology — and what does that signal?

**What good looks like:**
- A clear, named IAG BU with a specific KPI that this company moves (loss ratio, GWP, COR, NPS, retention)
- An existing pilot or commercial relationship with another carrier that validates the insurance use case
- A pathway to a pilot that does not require IAG to fundamentally change its processes

**Red flags:**
- Adjacency to insurance that is too thin to justify CVC involvement
- No existing insurance relationships — first insurer is expected to be IAG
- Strategic fit is entirely dependent on a single IAG BU champion who may move on

---

## Research Output Standards

For every dimension, the research output should include:
- A 2–4 sentence summary of the finding
- Specific evidence (names, numbers, dates, sources)
- A clear signal label: **Positive**, **Neutral**, **Negative**, or **Insufficient data**
- Any gaps flagged explicitly as [Unverified] or [Inference]
