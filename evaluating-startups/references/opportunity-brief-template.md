# Opportunity Brief Template

**Type:** Internal reference for deal evaluation  
**Audience:** Investment team, lead partner, IC members  
**Purpose:** Structured assessment of a startup opportunity after initial meeting or when moving to deeper evaluation. Captures commercial excitement, strategic rationale, risks, and next steps in a format ready for team discussion.  
**Stage:** Post-first-meeting or when entering deeper diligence  
**Note:** This is the IC-facing deliverable. The Evaluation Report is the research base behind it. The brief should be distilled, opinionated, and shareable without heavy editing.

---

## Canonical Structure

### 1. Recommendation / Commentary

High-level sentiment on the business. 2-3 sentences covering:

- What the company does (product, market, stage)
- Team size and geography
- How the ventures team encountered the deal (sourcing channel)
- Clear recommendation: proceed to evaluation, pass, or hold

**Tone:** Direct, opinionated. State the recommendation upfront. No hedge language.

---

### 2. What Gets Us Excited

3-4 key bullish points. Each point should be a short paragraph (2-4 sentences), not a single line.

Common categories (select the most relevant, not all):

- **Founders and Team:** Founder-market fit, domain expertise, team composition, hiring velocity
- **Product / Technology:** Differentiation, defensibility, technical moat, unique insight
- **Traction / Business Model:** Revenue, customer pipeline, growth rate, PMF signals, unit economics
- **Market Opportunity:** TAM, timing, tailwinds, regulatory catalysts, adoption inflection
- **Competitive Position:** Category leadership, white space, barriers to entry

**Standard:** Each point must be evidence-based. Cite specific data points, customer names, or metrics where available. Label anything unverified.

---

### 3. What Makes Us Pause

3-4 key risk points. Same depth as the bullish section. Be specific, not generic.

Common categories:

- **Defensibility:** Competitive threats, ease of replication, moat durability
- **Execution Risk:** Scaling challenges, operational complexity, team gaps
- **Market Risk:** Adoption uncertainty, regulatory headwinds, market timing
- **Valuation / Round Dynamics:** Pricing concerns, investor quality, cap table issues
- **Strategic Fit Risk:** Unclear integration path, dependency on single BU

**Standard:** Every risk should imply a question or action. If a risk is flagged, it should appear in the Critical Questions section or be addressed in Next Steps.

---

### 4. Why This Makes Sense for Us

3 key points connecting the opportunity to strategic context. This is the CVC-specific section. Must reference specific business units, KPIs, or capabilities.

For each point, address one or more of:

- **Short-term strategic value:** Direct impact on KPIs (loss ratio, GWP, expense ratio, CX/NPS, customer retention). Clear pathway to pilot, commercial partnership, or integration with specific BUs.
- **Long-term strategic value:** Reshapes risk value chain (underwriting, pricing, distribution, claims, CX). Builds future capabilities or optionality. Creates proprietary data, insights, or access not easily acquired later.
- **Internal validation:** Which stakeholders have been consulted? What was their feedback? Name teams, not individuals, in the written brief.

**Standard:** Every strategic rationale point must pass the "so what" test. Generic statements like "aligns with our innovation strategy" are not acceptable. Be specific about which part of the business benefits and how.

### 5. Round Considerations

Structured summary of the current funding round:

- **Round size and instrument** (equity, ConNote, SAFE)
- **Valuation** (pre-money and/or post-money)
- **Lead investor** (committed or in process)
- **Our allocation** (ticket size under consideration)
- **Key financial metrics** (ARR, burn, runway) if available
- **Cap table notes** (founder ownership, notable investors) if relevant

**Standard:** Flag if any data is unconfirmed. Note if further financial DD is required.

---

### 6. Critical Questions That Need Answered

4-6 open questions that must be resolved before an investment decision. These should be genuine unknowns, not rhetorical questions.

Good critical questions:

- Test a specific assumption (e.g., "Can the team reduce data dependencies to scale into new markets?")
- Address a flagged risk from Section 3
- Probe strategic fit depth (e.g., "How strategically valuable is X as a [use case] vs. a standalone investment?")
- Challenge market assumptions (e.g., "How big is the addressable segment within the broader TAM?")

**Standard:** Each question should be answerable through a specific action (customer call, data room review, expert interview, BU meeting). Vague questions are not useful.

### 7. Next Steps

Numbered list of concrete actions. Each item should specify:

- What needs to happen
- Who is involved (role/team, not individual names in the template)
- Implicit priority order (most important first)

Typical next steps include:

1. Internal stakeholder meetings (specific BUs)
2. Follow-up discussions with the founder/team
3. Data room access and commercial Q&A
4. Customer or investor reference calls
5. Formal DD process initiation

---

## Formatting Rules

- **Voice:** "Us" / "we" (team voice, not first person)
- **Length:** 1.5-2 pages when rendered as PDF. Concise but substantive.
- **Tone:** IC-adjacent. Should be shareable with the investment team without heavy editing.
- **Evidence:** Cite specific numbers, customer names, dates. Label unverified claims.
- **No filler:** Every sentence should carry information. No padding sections.
- **No em dashes.** Use commas, semicolons, or restructure the sentence.
