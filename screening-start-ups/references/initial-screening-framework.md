# Initial Screening Framework

**Version:** 1.0
**Last updated:** 2026-04-21
**Purpose:** Defines the evaluation lens for IAGFV initial company screens. Claude reads this before conducting any screen. Covers the seven assessment dimensions, signal quality standards, and research guidance for each.

---

## Evaluation Philosophy

An initial screen is a structured research snapshot, not a verdict. The goal is to form a calibrated lean — interesting or not — based on publicly available evidence, and to surface the questions that only a founder conversation can answer. A good screen is honest about what it cannot know.

Every company is assessed on seven dimensions: five universal (Team, Traction, TAM, Timing, Tech and Product, Investors) and two IAG-specific (Investors, Insurance Relevance). Each dimension has defined signal standards. Weak signals are not automatic passes — they are flags that require honest reporting.

---

## Dimension 1: Team

**The core question:** Do these founders have the right to win in this market?

**What to research:** Founder LinkedIn profiles, prior company history (exits, failures, tenure), press interviews, patents filed, academic background where relevant, advisory board composition.

**Strong signals:**
- Domain experts with direct prior experience in the problem space (insider knowledge, not just adjacent)
- Repeat entrepreneurs who have demonstrated the ability to execute through difficulty — hiring, pivoting, raising in a down market
- Founders who can show a deep, personal connection to the problem: lived experience, prior operational role, or a traceable moment of insight
- Team completeness for stage: technical depth if building a technical product, commercial experience if the GTM is the hard part
- Notable hires or advisors who fill genuine gaps rather than add credential value

**Weak signals:**
- No evidence of execution in difficult circumstances — only smooth-market success
- Obvious capability gaps for the problem type (e.g., a deep-tech product built by a team with no technical founder)
- No discernible connection to the problem: opportunistic market entry without domain knowledge
- Thin team with no signal of hiring velocity or advisor quality
- Founders with no verifiable track record beyond the current company

**Classification:** Apply the insider/outsider/hybrid framework. An insider has lived the problem. An outsider may bring fresh perspective but carries the burden of proving they understand the domain. Hybrids (technical outsider with domain insider co-founder, or vice versa) are often the most interesting — flag the composition explicitly.

## Dimension 2: Traction

**The core question:** Is there real evidence of business progress, or are they managing perception?

**What to research:** Company website metrics, press releases, customer announcements, app store ratings, Crunchbase/PitchBook milestones, hiring data on LinkedIn, industry coverage, founder conference talks.

**Strong signals:**
- Demonstrated historical growth in revenue, ARR, customer count, or platform usage — with directional numbers, not vague language ("rapid growth", "thousands of customers")
- Named customer logos with implied or stated commercial relationships (not just pilots or POCs)
- Geographic expansion, product line extension, or vertical entry as evidence of PMF beyond a beachhead
- Hiring velocity that outpaces the round size — evidence of scaling demand
- Retention signals: renewed contracts, case studies referencing multi-year relationships

**Weak signals:**
- No identifiable business progress: website-only presence with no external proof points
- Obfuscating language around metrics: "significant", "growing", "enterprise-grade" with no numbers attached
- Over-reliance on vanity indicators: accelerator logos, awards, speaking slots — none of these are traction
- Pilots announced but no conversion signals
- Customer count claims that cannot be cross-referenced anywhere publicly

**Note on stage calibration:** Expected traction varies by stage. A pre-seed company without revenue is normal. A Series B company without ARR disclosure or clear growth signals is a flag. Calibrate the standard to the round.

## Dimension 3: TAM (Market Attractiveness)

**The core question:** Is the market big enough, real enough, and accessible enough to support a venture-scale outcome?

**What to research:** Industry analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC), VC firm market memos, company-cited TAM claims (cross-reference against methodology), regulatory filings, adjacent market data for bottom-up triangulation.

**Strong signals:**
- Clear projected market size with a stated growth rate (YoY), ideally from multiple independent sources
- Evidence of unmet customer need: complaints, workarounds, incumbent underperformance
- Meaningful barriers to entry for followers: network effects, proprietary data, switching costs, regulatory moats, first-mover distribution advantage, IP
- Category still in formation or early growth phase — not yet mature or consolidating
- Both top-down and bottom-up market sizing approaches that converge on a plausible number

**Weak signals:**
- Fierce competition or oversaturation with no clear white space
- Demand uncertainty: unclear who pays, at what price, and why now
- Low switching costs that commoditise the product over time
- Regulatory exposure that could cap addressable market or impose structural costs
- TAM figures that are implausibly large or rely on "we just need 1% of a $100B market" logic

**Framing note:** Market size is necessary but not sufficient. A large market with a company that cannot capture a defensible share is not attractive. Assess TAM alongside competitive positioning.

## Dimension 4: Timing

**The core question:** Why is now the right moment for this company to exist?

**What to research:** Macro environment, sector-specific inflection points, regulatory changes, technology readiness (AI/ML maturity, API availability, infrastructure shift), public market comparables, funding environment for the category.

**Strong signals:**
- A clearly articulable "why now": a regulatory change, infrastructure unlock, cost curve inflection, or behavioural shift that makes the solution newly viable or urgent
- Favourable macro tailwinds: rising demand, cost pressure on incumbents, post-COVID acceleration of digital adoption in the sector
- Technology readiness inflection: the underlying technology has recently crossed the threshold that makes the product viable at scale
- Round terms and valuation that reflect the current funding environment — not priced as if it is 2021
- Regulatory tailwinds: new mandates, open data requirements, or licensing changes that create or accelerate demand

**Weak signals:**
- Unfavourable macro conditions that reduce urgency or purchasing power in the target segment
- Technology that is too early: requires infrastructure or behaviour change that is still 3-5 years out
- Category that had its moment: funded heavily in a prior cycle and has since cooled without a clear new catalyst
- Valuation expectations that are disconnected from current market benchmarks

## Dimension 5: Tech and Product

**The core question:** Is this product genuinely differentiated, and is there evidence it works?

**What to research:** Company website, product documentation, case studies, app store reviews, GitHub (for open-source components), patent filings, integration partner pages, third-party reviews (G2, Capterra for SaaS products), developer or API documentation.

**Strong signals:**
- Product capabilities that are specific, demonstrable, and not replicated on competitor websites
- Case studies with named, relevant customers that describe real outcomes — not generic testimonials
- Technical architecture or proprietary data assets that create durable differentiation (not just a better UX on top of existing infrastructure)
- API-first or platform design that enables integration — particularly relevant for insurance distribution
- Evidence of product iteration in response to customer feedback: version history, changelog, public roadmap signals

**Weak signals:**
- Feature set that mirrors competitor website language: "AI-powered", "real-time", "seamless" with no specifics
- No visible customer proof: website-only claims, no external validation
- Build-on-top-of-LLM product with no proprietary layer: replication risk is high
- Product that solves a workflow inefficiency but creates no lock-in
- Single-product company with no clear roadmap toward platform or expansion

---

## Dimension 6: Investors

**The core question:** Who has already evaluated and backed this company, and what does that signal?

**What to research:** Crunchbase, PitchBook, company press releases, investor portfolio pages, follow-on round participation.

**Strong signals:**
- Balanced mix of reputable institutional venture funds (Tier 1 generalist or specialist InsurTech/FinTech funds) and strategic investors with genuine operational value
- Existing investors following on in subsequent rounds: the strongest endorsement a current investor can give
- Strategic investors who bring distribution, not just capital (e.g., a carrier or broker taking a direct stake)
- Investor quality appropriate to stage: seed from angels and micro-VCs is normal; Series B from first-time funds only is a flag

**Weak signals:**
- Absence of any recognisable venture fund: friends-and-family or community round only, beyond the very earliest stage
- Prior investors not participating in the follow-on round with no stated explanation
- Exclusively strategic investors with no independent VC validation: can indicate a captive product rather than a standalone venture
- Signs of a bridge round dressed as a priced round

**IAG note:** Where IAG or a peer carrier is already a strategic investor, flag this explicitly — it changes the competitive dynamics of any further involvement.

## Dimension 7: Insurance Relevance [IAG-specific]

**The core question:** Is there a credible, direct connection to IAG's business — and can we articulate what it is?

**What to research:** Company's named insurance customers, carrier partnership announcements, industry awards or recognition from insurance bodies, founder background in insurance, product documentation targeting insurance use cases.

**Strong signals:**
- Large-scale commercial agreements with multiple insurance customers (not pilots, not POCs — live contracts)
- Named carrier logos on the website or in case studies
- Founders with prior roles at carriers, brokers, or insurance technology companies
- Product built specifically for insurance workflows: claims, underwriting, distribution, policy servicing
- Alignment with IAG's stated focus areas: InsurTech, Enterprise Tech, Mobility, Emerging Tech

**Weak signals:**
- Insurance relevance that is difficult to comprehend or poorly articulated even by the company itself
- A generic B2B product with a single insurance case study and no clear go-to-market priority in the sector
- Founders with no insurance background who are "exploring the insurance vertical"
- Product that could serve insurance but is primarily positioned elsewhere

**Signal rating:** Assign a rating of High, Medium, Low, or Unclear. Use Unclear only when the evidence is genuinely insufficient — not as a soft pass.
